# Pitch Deck

## The Core Principle

A pitch deck is not a document. It's a conversation starter.  
Its only job: make the audience want to have the next conversation.

Investors read hundreds of decks. Yours needs a clear story, not exhaustive data.

---

## Standard Pitch Deck Structure (10–12 slides)

### 1. Cover
- Company name, tagline (one line — what you do, for whom)
- Presenter name and contact
- "Confidential" if needed

### 2. Problem
- The pain, clearly stated
- Make the audience feel the pain, not just understand it
- Use a story or specific scenario if possible
- Size of the problem (how many people / how often / how bad)

### 3. Solution
- Your answer to the problem
- Keep it simple — one sentence
- Show the product if it exists (screenshot, demo gif)
- Don't over-explain the technology here

### 4. Market Size
- TAM / SAM / SOM (see market-research skill)
- Bottom-up calculation is more credible
- Show you understand which segment you're starting with

### 5. Business Model
- How do you make money?
- Pricing (if you have it)
- Unit economics: CAC, LTV, LTV:CAC ratio, payback period
- Revenue model: subscription / transaction / marketplace / SaaS / usage

### 6. Traction
- The most important slide if you have anything to show
- Revenue, users, retention, partnerships, LOIs, pilots
- Growth chart (MoM) > absolute numbers
- Customer logos if you have them
- If pre-traction: why now? What gives you conviction?

### 7. Competition
- Don't say "we have no competitors" — this kills credibility
- Positioning map (2 axes) or comparison table
- Clearly articulate your unfair advantage / differentiation

### 8. Product Roadmap
- What's been built, what's next
- Keep it high-level (Phases, not sprint plans)
- Tie roadmap to market expansion or key customer segments

### 9. Team
- Why are YOU the right people to solve this?
- Relevant experience, domain expertise, past wins
- Advisors with credibility add weight
- Be honest about gaps — and how you're addressing them

### 10. The Ask
- How much are you raising?
- What is it for? (Use of funds breakdown)
- What milestones will it get you to?
- What does success look like 18–24 months from now?

## One-Page Executive Summary

When a full deck isn't appropriate, use this:

```
Company: [Name]
Founded: [Year] | Stage: [Pre-seed/Seed/Series A]

Problem: [1–2 sentences]
Solution: [1–2 sentences]
Market: TAM $X | SAM $Y | Targeting [segment]
Business Model: [how you make money]
Traction: [key metrics]
Team: [names + 1-line bios]
Ask: Raising $X for [key milestones]
Contact: [email]
```

## Narrative Arc (Story Structure)

```
[Setup]     The world has changed / there's a problem that wasn't solvable before
[Conflict]  The status quo is broken / people are suffering / the cost is real
[Solution]  We built X — here's how it works
[Proof]     Here's evidence it works (traction, customers, data)
[Vision]    Here's where this is going (the big picture)
[Ask]       Here's what we need to get there
```

---

## What Kills Decks

- ❌ Too many slides (>15 for seed, >20 for any stage)
- ❌ Font smaller than 20pt
- ❌ Dense paragraphs — use bullet points and visuals
- ❌ Financials without context
- ❌ "No competition" slide
- ❌ Features list instead of benefits
- ❌ Jargon the audience doesn't know
- ❌ Missing the ask or making it vague

---

## Investor Questions to Prepare For

- Why now? Why hasn't this been done before?
- Why you? What's your unfair advantage?
- What's your moat? How do you defend this position?
- What does the unit economics look like at scale?
- Who's your biggest competitor, and how do you win against them?
- What happens if [big company] builds this?
- What's the biggest risk in this business?
- Walk me through your go-to-market for the first 100 customers.
