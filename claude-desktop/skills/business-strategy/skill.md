# Business Strategy

## Skill Stack

Pull these into the workflow when relevant:
- `market-research` — evidence-based TAM/SAM/SOM, competitor comparison, investor due diligence
- `competitive-platform-analysis` → `competitive-report-structure` — three-skill competitive pipeline for decision-grade reports
- `deep-research` — multi-source synthesis with citations when fresh data is needed
- `research-ops` — orchestration layer when multiple research lanes are needed
- `product-lens` — validate product direction before committing to strategy
- `marketing-campaign` — when strategy output flows into launch execution

## Guardrails

- Do not produce strategy without understanding the actual business context first
- Do not assert market size numbers without sources or bottom-up calculations
- Do not recommend entering a market without a beachhead segment named
- Separate: what we know (sourced fact), what we believe (inference), what we're betting on (hypothesis)
- A strategy that tries to serve everyone is not a strategy — push back if scope is too broad

## When to Activate

- "Help me plan our strategy for next year"
- "We're thinking of entering a new market"
- "What should we prioritize?"
- "How do we compete against [competitor]?"
- Any request for a strategic plan, competitive response, or market entry decision

## Operating Modes

### Mode 1: Situation Assessment

Before any strategy work, get the full picture:

```
1. What is the business / product? (one sentence)
2. What stage? (idea / PMF / scale / mature)
3. What does winning look like in 12 months? (specific metric)
4. What have you already tried? What failed?
5. What are the hard constraints? (budget, team, runway, regulation)
6. What are you willing NOT to do?
```

The last question separates real strategies from wishlists.

### Mode 2: Strategic Framework Selection

| Situation | Framework | Key Output |
|-----------|-----------|-----------|
| Evaluating market entry | Porter's Five Forces | Go/no-go + positioning requirements |
| Overcrowded market | Blue Ocean | Value innovation canvas |
| Portfolio of products/markets | BCG Matrix | Invest/harvest/divest decisions |
| Full business scan | SWOT + Ansoff Matrix | Strategic options ranked |
| Competitive response | Competitive-platform-analysis pipeline | Defensible competitor set + white-space |
| Annual planning | OKR alignment + Now/Next/Later roadmap | Prioritized initiative stack |

### Mode 3: Strategic Frameworks (Apply Rigorously)

#### Porter's Five Forces — Is This Market Worth Entering?

Assess each force as High / Medium / Low threat, then draw a conclusion:

```
Rivalry intensity:    [score] — [evidence, not opinion]
Threat of new entry:  [score] — [what barriers exist, or don't]
Threat of substitutes:[score] — [what else solves this problem]
Buyer power:          [score] — [how much leverage do customers have]
Supplier power:       [score] — [how much leverage do vendors have]

Verdict: [Enter and compete on X / Avoid / Enter via niche Y]
```

#### Blue Ocean — Create Uncontested Space

Eliminate-Reduce-Raise-Create (ERRC) grid. Requires knowing what competitors currently do:

```
Eliminate: [industry assumptions that add cost but no value to your target customer]
Reduce:    [factors well below industry standard — you're explicitly worse here]
Raise:     [factors well above industry standard — your moat]
Create:    [factors the industry doesn't offer — your new value]
```

Output: a strategy canvas comparing your curve to the industry average.

#### Ansoff Matrix — Growth Direction

```
Existing Products + Existing Markets → Market Penetration (safest)
New Products    + Existing Markets   → Product Development
Existing Products + New Markets      → Market Development
New Products    + New Markets        → Diversification (riskiest)
```

Identify which quadrant the proposed strategy lives in. Flag risk level accordingly.

## Strategic Output Templates

### One-Page Strategy

```
Company/Product: [name]
Stage: [seed / growth / scale]

Mission: [why we exist — one sentence]
12-Month Goal: [the one metric that defines winning]

Strategic Bets (choose max 3):
1. [Bet] — Rationale: [why this, why now] — Risk: [what could kill it]
2. [Bet] — Rationale: [why this, why now] — Risk: [what could kill it]
3. [Bet] — Rationale: [why this, why now] — Risk: [what could kill it]

Explicitly NOT doing this year:
- [What we're deprioritizing and why]

Key assumptions this strategy depends on:
- [If X is true, then this works. If X is false, we need to adjust.]
```

### Competitive Positioning Statement

```
For [specific target customer]
who [has this specific problem],
[company/product] is the [category]
that [key benefit — one thing only].
Unlike [primary alternative the customer actually uses today],
we [the meaningful difference, backed by evidence].
```

### Go-to-Market Strategy

```
ICP (Ideal Customer Profile):
  Who: [role, company type, size, geography]
  Pain: [specific problem, quantified if possible]
  Trigger: [what makes them look for a solution right now]
  Budget: [how they fund this type of purchase]

Value Proposition: "We help [ICP] [do job] so they can [outcome]."

Channel:
  Primary: [how they discover us]
  Secondary: [how we reach them at scale]
  Why this channel: [evidence it reaches the ICP]

Pricing:
  Model: [freemium / subscription / usage / one-time / enterprise]
  Price point: [with rationale — not just a number]

First 10 customers:
  Source: [exactly where they'll come from]
  Outreach: [what we'll say, and why they'll respond]
```

## Quality Gate

Before delivering strategy output:
- [ ] Every market size claim is sourced or labeled as estimate
- [ ] Competitor set is based on who customers actually compare, not assumption
- [ ] Beachhead segment is specific enough to name the first 10 customers
- [ ] Risks are named explicitly, not buried
- [ ] "Not doing" list exists — strategy without tradeoffs is not strategy
- [ ] Recommendation follows from the evidence — no recommendation that can't be defended
